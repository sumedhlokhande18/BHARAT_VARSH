History & Theory

The mathematical foundations behind Pingala's binary system and the Panchanga calendar science.

Pingala's Binary System (~300 BCE)

Pingala, a Sanskrit prosodist, described a binary numeration in his Chandashāstra — the science of poetic meters. He encoded numbers using short (laghu, 0) and long (guru, 1) syllable patterns, predating Leibniz's binary system by roughly 2,000 years.

His two operations — Nasta (encoding) and Uddhista (decoding) — form a complete binary arithmetic system. The encoding differs from modern binary by being 1-indexed, so all decoded values are offset by +1.

Nasta
Decimal → Pingala sequence. If n is even append 1 and halve; if odd append 0 and halve (rounding up).
Uddhista
Pingala sequence → Decimal. Reverse the sequence, decode as standard binary (MSB first), then add 1.

Panchanga — Five Limbs of the Day

Panchanga (पञ्चाङ्ग) is the traditional Hindu almanac. Each day is defined by five elements computed from the celestial longitudes of the Moon and Sun.

Tithi
Lunar day. Moon gains 12° on Sun per tithi. diff = moon−sun (mod 360°). Elapsed = ⌊diff÷12⌋, current = elapsed+1.
Karana
Half a tithi (6° arc). Elapsed = ⌊diff÷6⌋, current = elapsed+1.
Nakshatra
Lunar mansion. Moon traverses 27 nakshatras of 800 arc-minutes each.
Yoga
Sum of moon and sun arc-minutes ÷ 800 (27 divisions).
Vara
Day of the week — requires Julian Day Number; not computed here.

Inspiration

This project is dedicated to Pingala (पिङ्गल), the ancient Sanskrit prosodist whose Chandashāstra (~300 BCE) described the world's first known binary numeration system — two millennia before Leibniz. It is also inspired by the timeless tradition of Jyotisha, the Vedanga of astronomy, which gave us the Panchanga almanac still used across India today.
